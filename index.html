Inland Sea Oats

Scientific Name: Chasmanthium latifolium

Distribution: eastern United States, from Pennsylvania south to Florida and west to Arizona

Benefit to wildlife: seeds eaten by small mammals and granivorous birds, and its leaves grazed by larger mammals. Birds also use its stems and leaves as nesting material

Texas Prickly Pear

Scientific Name: Opuntia aciculata

Distribution: Dry shrubland biomes of southwestern United States, particularly near the Rio Grande in Webb County and northern Mexico

Benefit to wildlife: Provides an important food source, offering its pads and fruits to animals like deer, birds, and small mammals. Its dense, spiny structure also offers shelter and protection, creating a safe habitat for nesting birds and other small creatures

×

Crape Myrtle

Scientific Name: Lagerstroemia

Distribution: Places with warm, sunny climates such as the Southern United States, the Mediterranean, and Australia

Benefit for wildlife: Provides nectar for pollinators like bees and butterflies, supporting local insect populations. Its dense branches also offer perching and nesting sites for small birds

×

Bald Cypress

Scientific Name: Taxodium distichum

Distribution: Native to the southeastern United States, growing along the Atlantic Coastal Plain and inland through the Mississippi Valley

Benefit for wildlife: Provides important habitat for birds and other wildlife, with its branches used for nesting and its cavities sheltering animals

×

Chinese Wisteria

Scientific Name: Wisteria sinensis

Distribution: Native to China. Now widely invasive in the eastern U.S., particularly in the mid-Atlantic and southeastern states

Benefit for wildlife: Provides nectar for bees and other pollinators in spring. Its dense vines can offer cover and nesting sites for some birds and small animals

Castiron Plant

Scientific Name: Aspidistra elatior

Distribution: Native to the temperate regions of East Asia, but is widely cultivated in shaded gardens and indoors across temperate and subtropical regions worldwide

Benefit for wildlife: Provides dense ground cover that offers shelter and protection for small wildlife like insects and ground-dwelling birds

×

Coleus

Scientific Name: Plectranthus scutellarioides

Distribution: Native to Southeast Asia and Malaysia but is widely cultivated worldwide as an ornamental plant in tropical and subtropical regions

Benefit to wildlife: Attracts pollinators such as bees and butterflies with its colorful flowers, supporting local insect populations

×

Eastern Red Cedar

Scientific Name: Juniperus virginiana

Distribution: Eastern and central North America, found from Ontario and New England in the north, down to the Gulf Coast and across to Texas

Benefit to wildlife: Provides year-round shelter and nesting sites for birds, while its berries serve as an important food source for many bird species and small mammals

×

Buddhist Pine

Scientific Name: Podocarpus macrophyllus

Distribution: Native to southern China and Japan, but it is widely cultivated in subtropical and tropical regions around the world as an ornamental plant

Benefit to wildlife: Provides dense evergreen cover that offers shelter for birds and small animals, and its fleshy seed coverings are eaten by birds

×

Queen Victoria Agave

Scientific Name: Agave victoriae-reginae

Distribution: Native to the Chihuahuan Desert in northeastern Mexico. It is cultivated as an ornamental plant in arid and semi-arid regions, particularly in the Southwest

Benefit to wildlife: Its flowers produce nectar that attracts pollinators like bats, bees, and hummingbirds, supporting desert pollinator species

×

French Lavender

Scientific Name: Lavandula dentata

Distribution: Native to the Mediterranean region, particularly Spain and North Africa. In the US, it is commonly grown in warm, dry climates such as California and parts of the Southwest

Benefit to wildlife: Produces fragrant flowers rich in nectar, attracting bees, butterflies, and other pollinators throughout its blooming season.

Plant 12

Scientific Name: Solanum elaeagnifolium

Distribution: Native to the southwestern United States, Mexico, and parts of Central America

Benefit to wildlife: Provides pollen for bees and occasional berries for birds; also offers shelter for small animals in arid environments
